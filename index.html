01 · GIS SPATIAL ANALYSIS

Energy Coaching Access
in Massachusetts

Do wealthier municipalities have more access to clean energy coaching programs?

ArcGIS · Spatial Analysis

The Problem

Energy coaching programs that help residents understand and upgrade home heating systems are unevenly distributed across Massachusetts. Only 62 of 351 municipalities have coaching, covering just 39% of the state's population. The question: is access correlated with income?

Methods Used

Combined NEEP coaching data with MassDOR income data and MassGIS municipal boundaries. Used spatial joins, summary statistics, histogram analysis, and hotspot analysis in ArcGIS to compare income and population distributions between coaching and non-coaching municipalities.

Median income, with coaching$79,905
Median income, without coaching$45,421
Statewide median income$47,294

Policy Implications

Coaching is concentrated in affluent, densely populated suburbs, particularly MetroWest and North Shore, while lower-income communities with the highest need go underserved. Recommendations include expanding the MEM Grant model to fund regional coaching programs, grouping smaller municipalities to create sufficient demand, and providing implementation toolkits to communities without resources to build programs independently.

02 · AGENT-BASED MODELING

Simulating Urban Heat
Island Dynamics

How do spatial distributions of land cover types affect heat in urban environments?

NetLogo · ABM

The Problem

Urban heat islands (UHIs) emerge from complex interactions between impervious surfaces, vegetation, and spatial layout, but these dynamics are expensive and difficult to measure directly. The question: how do the quantity and spatial arrangement of land covers shape heat patterns in an urban environment?

Methods Used

Built an agent-based model in NetLogo simulating concrete patches, grass patches, and tree agents with calibrated heat inflow, outflow, and diffusion rates. Ran scenario testing across concrete quantity (25/50/75%), tree count (0/10/20), tree radius (1/3/5), and clustering (0/5/10) with 30 runs each to isolate the effect of each variable on mean temperatures.

25% concrete, overall mean temp83.3°F
75% concrete, overall mean temp95.8°F
Temp reduction, 0 to 20 trees−2.7°F

Policy Implications

The most striking finding: when concrete and grass are interleaved rather than segregated, concrete temperatures drop meaningfully, suggesting mixed land cover is more effective than isolated green space. Recommendations include greening transit corridors (e.g. grass beneath Green Line tracks, as in European streetcar systems), and using stormwater management infrastructure to replace impervious surfaces with vegetation in targeted areas.
